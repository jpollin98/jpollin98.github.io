Dr Joshua Pollin (He/Him)

Postdoctoral Scholar in Astrophysics
Oregon State University

Type Ia supernovae are precision cosmological tools, yet their origin remains unknown. I develop 3D radiative transfer models to link explosion physics directly to observables.

My aim is to unify the diversity of thermonuclear supernovae within a single framework, and extend full NLTE modelling across transients — from thermonuclear to core-collapse.

I grew up near Holywood, Northern Ireland, and went to Queen's University Belfast (QUB) to study a BSc in Physics before switching to an integrated Masters. It was during my Masters research project that I fell in love with transient astrophysics — and I haven't looked back since. My Masters and PhD were supervised by Professor Stuart Sim at QUB, where I developed a deep interest for radiative transfer and the physics of exploding stars.

A central focus of my PhD was the dynamically-driven double-degenerate double-detonation (D6) scenario, which I believe is the most promising progenitor for normal Type Ia supernovae. In this scenario, two white dwarfs in a binary system interact — both can explode, and as such are often called quadruple detonation events. The video below illustrates this remarkable process.

Type Ia supernovae underpin our measurement of the accelerating expansion of the Universe, yet their physical origin remains unresolved. This gap becomes increasingly critical as next-generation surveys such as LSST and the Roman Space Telescope deliver orders of magnitude more events. Resolving the progenitor question is not just a theoretical problem: uncertainties in SN Ia standardisation directly propagate into cosmological measurements and contributes to the current Hubble tension.

My research addresses this by connecting real, state-of-the-art 3D explosion models directly to observations through fully consistent NLTE radiative transfer simulations. Rather than relying on parameterised or 1D approximations, I model the full multidimensional ejecta structure, capturing the geometry, composition, and nucleosynthesis predicted by modern hydrodynamic simulations, and compute the resulting spectra and light curves from first principles.

A central goal is to determine whether the diversity of thermonuclear supernovae can be understood within a unified physical framework. By modelling how white dwarf mass, helium shell properties, and merger geometry shape observables across the full SN Ia population, I aim to establish which explosion channels dominate and how their nucleosynthetic signatures can be used to calibrate and correct for systematic biases in outher research areas.

A major priority going forward is driving observational investigations. I am especially passionate about JWST, which is transforming what is possible in supernova science. JWST opens up entirely new windows into ejecta composition and structure, and connecting my theoretical models directly to JWST observations is something I am actively pursuing. The variations predicted by my multidimensional calculations are already comparable in scale to features seen in recent JWST data, making this a particularly exciting time. If you are working with JWST observations of supernovae or explosive transients, I would very much like to collaborate.

More broadly, I am developing next-generation NLTE radiative transfer models that can be applied across the landscape of explosive transients. This includes extending detailed modelling to core-collapse supernovae, enabling direct comparisons between thermonuclear and massive-star explosions within a consistent framework, and building a programme in which radiative transfer becomes the primary tool for reading the nucleosynthetic record written in supernova light.

Different phases of supernova evolution probe different ejecta layers. Late-time calculations are especially powerful for connecting radiative transfer, nucleosynthesis, and the inner structure of the explosion.

For the first time, it is now possible to make observer-dependent NLTE spectral predictions for modern 3D explosion models. In Pollin et al. (2025), I presented the first such predictions, demonstrating that variations in line profiles arise directly from the ejecta geometry. This links spectral features to the underlying explosion structure, information inaccessible to 1D models. The resulting variations are substantial, comparable to those observed in recent JWST data.

Nebular-Phase Radiative Transfer

What sets the inner ejecta structure of thermonuclear explosions? I address this using nebular-phase radiative transfer, where line profiles and ionisation balance provide direct constraints on progenitor systems, asymmetries, and nucleosynthetic structure.

Unifying Type Ia Supernova Diversity

What drives the diversity of Type Ia supernova observables? I investigate how white dwarf mass, helium shells, and merger geometry shape spectra and light curves, with the broader aim of unifying normal events and their subclasses within a single physical picture.

Polarisation and Ejecta Geometry

How can we robustly identify three-dimensional structure in supernova ejecta? Polarisation provides a complementary probe, and by modelling orientation-dependent signatures I test how explosion asymmetries map onto observables that cannot be captured in spherical symmetry.

Modelling Across the Explosive Transients Landscape

Can the same modelling framework be applied to both thermonuclear and massive-star explosions? At Oregon State University, I am conducting hydrodynamic explosions with the goal of extending detailed NLTE radiative transfer calculations beyond Type Ia supernovae to core-collapse events, thereby building a broader programme for interpreting explosive transients within a consistent radiative-transfer framework.

My work combines code development, large-scale simulation campaigns, and observational interpretation. Selected contributions include:

  • Developed the first fully 3D NLTE nebular-phase radiative transfer simulations for Type Ia supernovae.
  • Produced the first multidimensional photospheric-phase synthetic observables of dynamical mergers.
  • Performed the first 3D polarisation calculations for double-degenerate explosion models.
  • Helped secure and exploit approximately 95 million CPU hours across major national and international HPC facilities.
  • Contributed to successful large-scale proposals on DiRAC, JUWELS, and SuperMUC-NG, translating computational resources into first-author and collaborative publications.
  • Expanded my research programme to include core-collapse supernova modelling and nucleosynthesis post-processing.

Other highlights include co-investigator status on a successful ESO VLT FORS2 proposal, invited talks at Trinity College Dublin and Leiden, and being an integral part of organising and chairing the session "Explosive Transients in the Present and Future Sky" at the National Astronomy Meeting (NAM) 2025 in Durham, a session composed entirely of early-career researchers.

I believe strongly that astronomy and astrophysics is for everyone. Science thrives when it is open, inclusive, and collaborative, and I am committed to contributing to an environment where people from all backgrounds feel welcome and supported. We are stronger together, and diversity of perspective makes for better science and scientists.
