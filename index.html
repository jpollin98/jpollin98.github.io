Dr Joshua Pollin

Postdoctoral Scholar in Astrophysics
Oregon State University

Type Ia supernovae are used as precision cosmological tools, yet their physical origin remains unresolved. I develop multidimensional radiative transfer models to link explosion physics directly to observable signatures.

My aim is to unify the diversity of thermonuclear supernovae within a single framework, and to extend full NLTE modelling across transients — from thermonuclear to core-collapse.

Despite their central role in cosmology, the physical origin and diversity of Type Ia supernovae remain unresolved. My work addresses this by linking multidimensional explosion physics directly to observable spectra through fully consistent NLTE radiative transfer.

A central goal of my research is to determine whether the diversity of thermonuclear supernovae can be understood within a unified physical framework. By modelling the full 3D structure of the ejecta, I connect explosion physics and nucleosynthesis directly to spectra, light curves, and late-time diagnostics.

More broadly, I am developing next-generation NLTE radiative transfer models that can be applied across the landscape of explosive transients. This includes extending detailed modelling to core-collapse supernovae, enabling direct comparisons between thermonuclear and massive-star explosions within a consistent framework.

Different phases of supernova evolution probe different ejecta layers. Late-time calculations are especially powerful for connecting radiative transfer, nucleosynthesis, and the inner structure of the explosion.

For the first time, it is now possible to make observer-dependent NLTE spectral predictions for modern 3D explosion models. In Pollin et al. (2025), I presented the first such predictions, demonstrating that variations in line profiles arise directly from the ejecta geometry. This links spectral features to the underlying explosion structure—information inaccessible to 1D models. The resulting variations are substantial, comparable to those observed in recent JWST data.

Nebular-Phase Radiative Transfer

What sets the inner ejecta structure of thermonuclear explosions? I address this using nebular-phase radiative transfer, where line profiles and ionisation balance provide direct constraints on progenitor systems, asymmetries, and nucleosynthetic structure.

Unifying Type Ia Supernova Diversity

What drives the diversity of Type Ia supernova observables? I investigate how white dwarf mass, helium shells, and merger geometry shape spectra and light curves, with the broader aim of unifying normal events and their subclasses within a single physical picture.

Polarisation and Ejecta Geometry

How can we robustly identify three-dimensional structure in supernova ejecta? Polarisation provides a complementary probe, and by modelling orientation-dependent signatures I test how explosion asymmetries map onto observables that cannot be captured in spherical symmetry.

Modelling Across the Explosive Transients landscape

Can the same modelling framework be applied to both thermonuclear and massive-star explosions? At Oregon State University, I am conducting hydrodynamic explosions with the goal of extending detailed NLTE radiative transfer calculations beyond Type Ia supernovae to core-collapse events, thereby building a broader programme for interpreting explosive transients within a consistent radiative-transfer framework.

My work combines code development, large-scale simulation campaigns, and observational interpretation. Selected contributions include:

  • Developed the first fully 3D NLTE nebular-phase radiative transfer simulations for Type Ia supernovae.
  • Produced the first multidimensional photospheric-phase synthetic observables of dynamical mergers.
  • Performed the first 3D polarisation calculations for double-degenerate explosion models.
  • Helped secure and exploit approximately 95 million CPU hours across major national and international HPC facilities.
  • Contributed to successful large-scale proposals on DiRAC, JUWELS, and SuperMUC-NG, translating computational resources into first-author and collaborative publications.
  • Expanded my research programme to include core-collapse supernova modelling and nucleosynthesis post-processing.

Other highlights include co-investigator status on a successful ESO VLT FORS2 proposal, invited talks at Trinity College Dublin and Leiden, and organising a National Astronomy Meeting session on explosive transients.
